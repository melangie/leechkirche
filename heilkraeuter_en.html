Medicinal herbs in the Leechkirche

If you look carefully inside the church, you can recognise various Gothic plant capitals high up on the base of the vault. Here, the importance of the medieval clergy for the theology and mysticism of the time becomes visible.

The leaf decoration on the capitals has its predecessor in the ancient Corinthian column. The decoration of the column with acanthus ornamentation set it apart from the two other main styles of the Doric and Ionic columns. While Charlemagne still had the classical Corinthian motif used in his Palatine Chapel, the ornamentation underwent profound changes as early as the Carolingian period. But even the German rulers of the 12th century still used the acanthus leaf as a symbol of power.

In the 13th century, the use of ornamentation while maintaining the basic architectural form was increasingly replaced by the dissolution of this original form. In the sense of the church as a symbol of heaven, the leaf and flower capitals fulfil the role of the blossoming meadows of paradise. The most magnificent examples include Reims Cathedral, Mainz Cathedral and Naumburg Cathedral.

In addition to its symbolic significance, plant ornamentation also reflects the emergence of a new world view in which nature became the subject of scientific research. The scholars Albert Magnus and his pupil Thomas Aquinas made Aristotle's writings accessible, which led to the scientification and systematisation of nature. Each plant was now given its own value and became worthy of representation for its own sake. This explains the wealth of forms and the naturalness of the plant depictions.

The plant ornaments on the Leech Church in Graz (1283-1293) were also created at the same time. They are therefore probably the earliest realistic depictions of plants of this kind in Austria. The French models of the 1250s, which were conveyed by German sculptors, served as orientation. The Teutonic Knights probably also played a role here, as their church of St Elisabeth in Marburg an der Lahn also features similar leaf motifs. The use of plants also shows a connection with the hospital tradition and the associated medical knowledge of the order. The knowledge of the monks could therefore have found its way into the capital decoration of their churches and also determined the choice of plants.
